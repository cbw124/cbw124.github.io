Formal language uses symbols to represent abstractions. A symbol designates either an indeterminate particular or a determinate individual.
\(x: \mathbb{R}\) \(\pi: \mathbb{R}\) \(f(x) :\equiv x + \pi\) \(f:\mathbb{R}\to \mathbb{R}\)

Substitution is determination.
\(M[N/x] :\equiv ``\text{ in term } M \text{, define } x \text{ to be } N"\)
The canonical form is function application.
\(f(\pi) = \pi + \pi = \tau\)

This simple act is the heartbeat of computation.

However, mathematics and computer science have different perspectives of symbolic representation. Mathematics in general uses symbols informally; an exposition simply chooses enough distinct symbols to reason precisely. In computer science, however, the representation is completely formal, and in fact crucial - symbols are either necessary placeholders, or actual references to data.
